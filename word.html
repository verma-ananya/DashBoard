4. Statistical Measures

4.1 Mean (μ)

The mean is the average of the numbers: a calculated "central" value of a set of numbers. The mean is calculated by adding up all the numbers, then dividing by how many numbers there are.

4.2 Standard Deviation (σ)

The standard deviation is a statistic that tells you how tightly all the various examples are clustered around the mean in a set of data. When the examples are tightly bunched together, and the bell-shaped curve is steep, the standard deviation is small. When the examples are spread apart, and the bell curve is relatively flat, that tells you have a relatively large standard deviation.

4.3 Confidence Level (CL)

The confidence level is the plus-or-minus figure usually reported in newspaper or television opinion poll results. For example, if you use a confidence level of 4 and 47% percent of your sample picks an answer you can be "sure" that if you had asked the question of the entire relevant population between 43% (47-4) and 51% (47+4) would have picked that answer. 4.4 Confidence Interval (CI)

A confidence interval is a range of values within which the standard deviation will be found with a known probability if the estimation procedure is repeated many times.

5. SWOT(Beta)

SWOT analysis is a strategic planning technique used to help a person or organization identify the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats related to business competition or project planning.

SWOT stands for Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats.
  • Strengths: Attributes and resources that support a successful outcome.
  • Weaknesses: Attributes and resources that work against a successful outcome.
  • Opportunities: External factors that the entity can capitalize on or use to its advantage
  • Threats: External factors that could jeopardize the entity's success.
Note: Traditionally Strengths and Weaknesses referred to internal attributes while Opportunities and Risks referred to external attributes. We have ignored this division as we believe all SWOT criteria are useful in both external and internal strategic analysis.

Therefore, our system has been developed to identify and separate possible external SWO's in the form of FORECASTS that are relevant to your interest topic. Clicking on each SWOT category will highlight those our robot thinks fit closest to an external SWOT categorization.

You can separately create an equivalent internal SWOT on your organization by adding a project here. Then you can compare the two analyses for where your major opportunities and threats lie and then develop your strategic response, again by developing the project you have added here.

Importantly, one organizatio's strength is possibly another's weakness and an opportunity for one is maybe a threat to another. So, when reading the external SWOT's consider both the positive and negative futures that might arise from the presented forecasts depending on whether you are the attacker, defender or just an observer.

Since this is a new and unique, beta service she may occasionally wrongly assign an opportunity to a threat or a strength to a weakness or vice versa. However, that should not stop you from considering both sides of the change. It's your thoughts and revelations that count from this analysis; not exact classification, every time.
